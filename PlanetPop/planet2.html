Venus: Earth’s Scorching Twin

Venus, the second planet from the Sun, is similar in size, mass, and composition to Earth—earning the nickname “Earth’s twin.” Yet its surface is a hellish world: surface temperatures of ~ 465 °C and atmospheric pressures ~ 90 atm make it inhospitable and geophysically unique. Size & Structure: With a radius of ~6,052 km—97% of Earth’s—the planet is slightly smaller but nearly identical in density, implying a similar iron-rich core structure. Geologic mapping from Magellan reveals ~80% basaltic plains, shield volcanoes, tesserae highlands, and coronae—indicative of extensive volcanism and tectonic deformation.¹²³⁴ Composition: Dominated by CO₂ (~96.5%) with cloud layers of sulfuric acid droplets. Super‑rotation at cloud tops results in winds of 100 m/s—50× faster than surface rotation—producing polar vortices and Hadley circulation.³⁵ Atmospheric Evolution: Modeling suggests multi-phase evolution—possibly early magma ocean desiccation, a later temperate period, and eventual runaway greenhouse—shaped by volcanic outgassing, escape, and surface–atmosphere transfers.⁶⁷ Water History: Atmospheric isotopic studies show extremely high D/H ratios—up to ~1,500× Earth's—implying massive hydrogen loss and a historically wetter Venus.⁸ However, recent work on volcanic gas isotopes shows modern outgassed volatile waters  6%—much drier than Earth’s—supporting a “dry” interior and no lasting oceans.⁹¹⁰ Radar re-analysis of Magellan data reveals lava flows in Sif Mons and Niobe Planitia, consistent with active shield volcanism and comparable to Earth’s style—supporting the idea that Venus remains geologically active.¹¹ Venera (USSR) landers (1967–84) measured CO₂-rich air, high surface T/P, and imaged the surface.¹² Venus Express (ESA, 2006–2015) provided long-duration atmospheric and cloud dynamics data.¹³ Magellan (NASA, 1990–92) mapped topography and radar-imaged geology and volcanism.¹²¹¹ DAVINCI (NASA, launch ~2029): atmospheric descent probe to sample gases, noble gases, isotopes 2029–2031.¹⁴ VERITAS (NASA, launch ~2031): high-res radar, spectroscopy, and geodesy to map tectonics and age-date volcanic resurfacing.¹⁵ Venus's dense, acidic clouds pose challenging environments, but chemical anomalies—such as PH₃, unexplained absorbers, and O₂ out of equilibrium—provide motivation for astrobiological exploration via atmospheric probes and future aerobots like balloon platforms.⁶¹⁶ Venus provides a cautionary counterpart to Earth—showing how similar planets can diverge due to runaway greenhouse effects, volatile loss, and tectonic regimes. Ongoing and upcoming missions like DAVINCI and VERITAS will shed vital light on its evolution, geology, and atmospheric chemistry.
